### CORTEX BioSciences: GROUD TRUTH

Teams are given a knowledge base of beliefs, a structured graph in which every claim carries a confidence value and a record of the evidence behind it, together with a stream of new experimental results that arrive one at a time. Their job is to build a system that updates those beliefs correctly as the evidence comes in: revising when a result is real and well replicated, holding firm when it is hype or fraud, and flagging results that fall outside what the model was built to represent. Some incoming results contain instructions designed to overwrite the knowledge base directly, and the system must never let text rewrite what it knows. Most systems fail in one of two ways. They believe whatever they were told last, or they stubbornly ignore everything. The entire challenge is doing neither. No science background is required; the knowledge base can be read as an abstract graph of states and claims. 

**Why CORTEX is sponsoring Hack the 6ix**
CORTEX builds a research intelligence platform for science. At its core is a knowledge graph that models what is currently believed about a domain, with confidence and provenance attached to every claim. Language models  sit on top and propose hypotheses, but they are never allowed to write to the graph directly. The graph is the  source of truth and the models are advisors. We call that boundary the firewall. 

The unsolved part is keeping that graph correct as new results stream in: updating by the right amount,  resisting weak or fraudulent evidence, extending the model when something falls outside it, and never letting  persuasive text overwrite ground truth. That problem is not solved at production quality anywhere, and it is the center of what we work on.

GROUND TRUTH is a self-contained version of it. We are explicit with participants that nobody solves this in a  weekend, and that we are looking for the right structure and the right instincts. That honesty is deliberate. It  sets expectations correctly, and it attracts the kind of participant we want to meet.

**What is being tested**
Four capabilities, published openly in the repository so teams know the rubric from the first minute.

| **Axis** | **Weight** | **What it measures** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Firewall integrity  | Pass/fail  gate | Untrusted text can never mutate the knowledge base. Any  successful injection disqualifies, regardless of other scores. |
| Calibrated belief revision  | 40  | Move confidence in proportion to evidence strength, read from  structured provenance rather than tone. Narrowing a belief beats  deleting it. |
| Skepticism without  gullibility | 25  | Hold firm against extraordinary claims with thin support, and  resolve them cleanly when they fail to replicate. |
| Out-of-distribution  detection | 35  | Separate a genuine contradiction from something the model was  never built to represent. Precision matters as much as recall. |

Revision is graded on the shape of the confidence trajectory, small on weak evidence, large on strong, near  zero on noise, rather than on hitting exact numbers. There is no single correct value for how far a rational agent should update, and the rubric is calibrated accordingly.
Two design details are worth noting, because they are what make the scoring hard to game. One of the false  alarms in the hidden set is fabricated and has no real-world counterpart, so a system that recognizes the  answer from its training data gets no help; only genuine provenance-based skepticism passes it. The hidden set  also contains a near miss that looks exotic but is an ordinary contradiction, so a team that flags everything as  out of model scores as badly as a team that flags nothing.

**Who should enter**
The challenge rewards background in machine reasoning, knowledge graphs, probabilistic modeling, or LLM  agent systems. It does not reward front-end polish or product design. We ask that the marketing signal this  clearly so the right participants self-select. Setting the expectation up front improves both submission quality  and the entrant experience.
Prior science knowledge is not required, and we would like that stated prominently. It is the single most  common reason a strong candidate would wrongly skip this challenge.

**What teams bring**
Laptops and their own code. Python 3.10+ and the standard library are sufficient. No special hardware, no  model costs, no external data.

### Blockchain for Good (BGA):  AI Trading Strategies for Financial Inclusion

This track asks university teams to build an AI-assisted trading or investing strategy that directly tackles a real Blockchain for Good problem — financial exclusion, opacity in AI-driven advice, exploitation of inexperienced investors, information asymmetry, or barriers to market access. It's a simplified version of the professional AI Trading & Strategy track: the infrastructure bar is lower (no on-chain deployment, no live capital, no custom multi-agent systems required), but the intellectual bar is not. Submissions are expected to name a specific harm, define who it affects, and build a strategy whose design choices exist because of that harm — not a generic trading idea with a good-intentions label attached afterward.

**Evaluation Methodology**

Judging weighs six criteria, totaling 100 points, with impact-related categories carrying the most weight:

- **Problem Identification & Ethos Fit (25 pts)** — is the named Blockchain-for-Good problem specific and well-evidenced, not generic?
- **Mechanism of Impact (20 pts)** — does the strategy's design causally address that problem? Judges trace a line from "this is the harm" to "this is the feature that prevents it."
- **Strategy Logic & Reasoning (20 pts)** — defined inputs, decision rule, and outputs, with reasoning for why the strategy generalizes beyond the test data rather than just fitting it.
- **Risk Management (15 pts)** — quantified sizing, drawdown, or stop-loss rules with a visible effect on the backtest, not just a stated intention.
- **Backtest / Evidence (10 pts)** — tested against real historical data (a spreadsheet or basic script is enough), with limitations honestly reported.
- **Transparency & Presentation (10 pts)** — clear to a non-technical judge, including the impact argument itself.

Ethos and mechanism-of-impact together account for 45% of the score, ahead of strategy and risk combined — so a technically polished but generically-motivated project scores below one built explicitly around solving a named problem, even with a rougher backtest.

**What We Want to See**

**Projects should pick one real harm and one real audience, and let every design decision trace back to that choice.** Strong candidates look like:

A concentration-limit strategy for first-time investors — targets the harm of inexperienced investors overexposing themselves to a single volatile asset; the rule caps allocation and explains itself in plain language at every trade.

A panic-selling circuit breaker — targets the harm of AI/algorithmic tools amplifying herd behavior during crashes; detects rapid sell-offs and imposes a short cooling-off delay rather than executing immediately.

A transparent robo-advisor for underbanked users — targets the harm of opaque AI advice that users can't interrogate; every recommendation comes with a plain-language reason, and the strategy is intentionally conservative given the audience's low risk tolerance.

A fairness rule for shared/club portfolios — targets the harm of one member's preferences dominating a group's shared capital; flags or blocks trades that would concentrate risk toward a single participant's favorite asset.

A calibration-based advisor — targets the harm of overconfident AI signals misleading retail users; scores its own historical accuracy and only surfaces recommendations above a confidence threshold, showing its track record openly.

In each case, the "AI for Good" framing isn't a paragraph in the intro — it's the reason the strategy has the shape it does.
